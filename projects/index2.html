PROJECTS

01) PLANT TO STOP POVERTY (PSP)

Plant to stop poverty is a massive tree planting project that intends to raise and plant eight million trees along North east coast of Tanzania and areas adjacent. Following ecological importance of the region, increasing poverty intensity and risk for species extinction PSP is designed to offer angled solutions for both improved livelihood and conservation. In PSP 6,400,000 trees raised are from 72 well studied indigenous species to restore lost biodiversity and stabilize species richness in scratched natural forests. 10% of the indigenous trees will be “key stones” especially figs to cover water sheds and water source areas including invaded banks of Pangani and Zigi rivers highly affected by frequent bank erosion. Degradation in rivers threatens the depths and sizes, access to clean water and hydroelectric power production (The only source of power in the region). The remaining 20% are agroforestry and grafted fruit trees to freely be offered to peasants to cut down overdependence on forest reserves by providing food, wood, income, soil nutrients, forage and habitat for other living organisms. Communities living within Amani nature reserve and significant shoreline of Tanga, Pangani and Bagamoyo are central targets to facilitate the goal. PSP campaign uses an incorporated approach derived from several environmental scientific findings to assist peasants in rural areas with right knowledge on tree species to stop hunger, poverty and protect their environment. A key challenge is the fact that peasants are obliged to plant trees in similar smaller land portions they use to produce other crops families rely on for food. By just planting any trees the land gets more squeezed for other food crops to reward. To overcome such challenges the following are addressed. In the early phase providing 100 trees to each selected farmer Conducting seminars and trainings on right species, planting information and smart agriculture. Revitalizing degraded and isolated soils to expand productive village lands using cheap sustainable technologies. Encouraging the use of biological methods in pest management and soils improvement to sustain the soil quality. Reaching out schools and all educational institutions to provide environmental education, awareness creations for eco-friendly practices.

02) IMPROVEMENT OF SOCIAL WELFARE OF THE DISADVANTAGED & DISABLED GROUPS

Young generation especially girls in rural areas have always been subjected to harsh learning environment in either homes or schools. They are exposed to gender demarcations and stereotypes which mostly stop them from reaching their educational careers in all schooling levels poverty being the case. Orphans, single parented and children from extremely poor families are often denied from right to education as a result of poverty and unfriendly learning environment. Five percent of the tree planting costs is usually used to fund and undertake several supportive activities for these needy children in the region where tree planting activities are taking place. The main intention is to assist them through their education career to higher stages where they realize how to utilize their potentials. Basic needs such as food, school contributions, clothing, transport, power and stationeries for screened children are well handled for them to achieve their dreams. Supporting these children goes with improving rural infrastructures such as construction of classes and improvement of health provision facilities as laying a foundation towards a better community. Tree nurseries established accommodate most of the unemployed community members who work on day-to-day basis. To an extent spending 6 to 8 hours in a tree nursery has reduced the idle labor groups associated with environmental degradation. Together with the rest agroforestry tree support offered to each house hold is an integral toward economic liberation in the near future through produce expected.

03) ECONOMIC EMPOWERMENT.

The main challenge we address is resolving extreme poverty in the countryside villages with its impact contributing to the loss of biodiversity. Life opportunities in the region are limited to rainfall overdependence agriculture, fishing and Charcoal making. In the meantime, fishing and agriculture have provided less due to climate change effects on rainfall trends. To still earn living the community has turned to the only remaining hope, the charcoal business which made hundreds of species extinct (The IUCN global red list). Ministerial statistics show a 30% growth of coastal charcoal business in 2018. Researchers conclude how extreme poverty and hunger in the community have keyed the notable ecological damage in this larger portion of the 23rd Word biodiversity hotspot. By applying smart agricultural techniques such as budding and grafting farmers are insured to harvest higher quality nuts, fruits and fodder in a less than twelve months period. These produce from agroforests will stimulate entrepreneurial ventures to boost businesses and economies in rural areas all while stopping deforestation.
